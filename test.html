About the Group

Our research connects electronic structure theory, machine learning, and open-system quantum dynamics to answer fundamental questions in electrochemistry, quantum materials, and excited-state chemistry.

We aim to develop methods that connect microscopic mechanisms to experimentally measurable response, with strong ties to catalysis, spectroscopy, and quantum technologies.

Research areas

We work on a range of quantum-mechanical problems motivated by excited-state phenomena. This research occurs at the fascinating interface of physical chemistry, condensed-matter physics, and materials science.
Image

Quantum dynamics and spectroscopy

Building on modern theories of quantum dynamics, we develop powerful simulation techniques for nonequilibrium and time-resolved spectroscopies. These tools enable the accurate simulation of large and complex systems, providing new insights into excited-state structure and dynamics.

Image

Emerging materials

We are actively exploring the excited-state behavior of fundamentally interesting and technologically promising materials, especially those that are anisotropic, layered, or low-dimensional. Particular materials of interest include conjugated polymers, organic molecular crystals, and quasi-two-dimensional inorganic semiconductors.

Image

Condensed-phase electronic structure

Aiming towards highly accurate but insightful descriptions of electronic excitations, we formulate and apply electronic structure methods adapted for the condensed phase. Some of our favorite tools are low-energy effective theories, many-body diagrammatics, and coupled-cluster techniques.

About Sohang

Sohang Kundu is an incoming Assistant Professor in the Department of Chemistry and Biochemistry at The Ohio State University. His research lies at the interface of theoretical chemistry, condensed-matter physics, and materials science, with a focus on developing predictive numerical methods for complex molecular and condensed-phase systems.

He develops theories and simulation strategies for the coupled dynamics of electrons, nuclei, spins, and electromagnetic fields. His work spans electrochemical interfaces, magnetic quantum materials, nonequilibrium spectroscopy, and open-system quantum dynamics, with an emphasis on connecting microscopic mechanisms to experimentally observable response.
